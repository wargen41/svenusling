  • When [Don Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] was hired as director, he was considering [Audie Murphy][/p?id=1063] for the role of Scorpio. [Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] thought it would be a nice irony to have a genuine war hero and heroic screen icon known for playing clean-cut characters playing a psychopathic killer. [Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] offered [Murphy][/p?id=1063] the role, but [Murphy][/p?id=1063] died in a plane crash on 28 May 1971 prior to making his final decision.
  • After the film was released, actor [Andrew Robinson][/p?id=Andrew%20Robinson] received several death threats, and had to get an unlisted phone number.
  • When Universal let go of the script, Warner Bros. purchased it with a view to cast [Frank Sinatra][/p?id=1302]. [Sinatra][/p?id=1302] was interested, however he had broken his wrist during the filming of [The Manchurian Candidate][/f?id=Hj%C3%A4rntv%C3%A4ttad] (1962) eight years previously, and during contract negotiations, he found the large handgun too unwieldy. Additionally, his father had recently passed away, and [Sinatra][/p?id=1302] decided he wanted to do some lighter material. After [Sinatra][/p?id=1302] dropped out, Warner Bros. considered [Marlon Brando][/p?id=1616] for the role, but never officially approached him. Next, they offered it to [Steve McQueen][/p?id=1904] and then [Paul Newman][/p?id=1727], both of whom turned it down. [Newman][/p?id=1727] however suggested [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] as a possible star.
  • In real life, [Andrew Robinson][/p?id=Andrew%20Robinson] is a pacifist who despises guns. In the early days of principal photography, [Robinson][/p?id=Andrew%20Robinson] would flinch violently every time he fired. Director [Don Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] was forced to shut down production for a time and sent [Robinson][/p?id=Andrew%20Robinson] to a school to learn to fire a gun convincingly. However, he still blinks noticeably when he shoots. [Robinson][/p?id=Andrew%20Robinson] was also squeamish about filming the scene where he verbally and physically abuses several schoolchildren, and the scene where he racially insults the man he pays to beat him up ([Raymond Johnson][/p?id=Raymond%20Johnson]).
  • As is well known, [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] directed the scene with the suicide jumper ([Bill Couch][/p?id=Bill%20Couch]). However, it is often claimed he directed the scene only because [Don Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] was ill. This is inaccurate. [Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] was indeed ill, and wasn't on the set, but [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] had always been scheduled to direct that scene, due to the difficult logistics of getting the actors, director, camera-man and sound-man all together on the top of a small ledge. In the shooting schedule, 6 nights had been set aside for the shooting of the scene. [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] told the studio he could shoot it in two nights. In the end, he shot the entire scene in one night.
  • After [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] and [Don Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] came on board the project, they hired writer Dean Riesner to work on the script. In his first rewrite, the bank robbery scene ends with Harry not pointing the gun at the robber, but placing it against his own temple. He pulls the trigger, laughs and then walks away. [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] and [Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel] both felt this was too extreme, even for Harry Callahan.
  • Initially, Warner Bros. wanted either [Sydney Pollack][/p?id=1914] or [Irvin Kershner][/p?id=Irvin%20Kershner] to direct. [Kershner][/p?id=Irvin%20Kershner] was eventually hired when [Frank Sinatra][/p?id=1302] was still set to star, but when [Sinatra][/p?id=1302] dropped out, so too did [Kershner][/p?id=Irvin%20Kershner].
  • [Robert Mitchum][/p?id=1815], [John Wayne][/p?id=1504], [Steve McQueen][/p?id=1904] and [Burt Lancaster][/p?id=1120] all claimed to have turned the film down.
  • The first of five movies starring [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] as Inspector "Dirty" Harry Callahan.
