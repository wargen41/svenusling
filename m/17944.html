  • Sir [Christopher Lee][/p?id=1169] (Saruman) read "The Lord of the Rings" once a year until his death in 2015, and had done so since the year it was published. He was also the only member of the cast and crew ever to have met J.R.R. Tolkien.
  • Gandalf's painful encounter with a ceiling beam in Bilbo's hobbit-hole was not in the script. Sir [Ian McKellen][/p?id=1387] banged his forehead against the beam accidentally. [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] thought [McKellen][/p?id=1387] did a great job "acting through" the mistake, and kept it in.
  • [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] gave one of the rings used in the movies to both [Elijah Wood][/p?id=1272] and [Andy Serkis][/p?id=1039] as a gift when the shoot was finished. They both thought they had the only one.
  • [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977] did his own stunts. He also insisted on using only the real steel sword, instead of a significantly lighter aluminum sword, or safer rubber sword, which were manufactured for battle scenes and stunts.
  • When Pippin is being hit with the apples after asking about second breakfast, it is [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977] chucking the apple at his head. They had to shoot the scene sixteen times to get it just right, and [Billy Boyd][/p?id=Billy%20Boyd] says he believes [Mortensen][/p?id=1977] enjoyed himself immensely.
  • The cast often had to fly to remote shoot locations by helicopter. [Sean Bean][/p?id=1865] (Boromir) was afraid of flying and would only do it when absolutely necessary. When they were shooting the scenes of the Fellowship crossing the snowy mountains he'd spend two hours every morning climbing from the base of the mountain to the set near the top, already dressed as Boromir. The crew being flown up could see him from their helicopters.
  • For high-tech tasks, a computer program called MASSIVE made armies of CGI orcs, elves, and humans. These digital creations could "think" and battle independently, identifying friend or foe, thanks to individual fields of vision. [Peter Jackson's][/p?id=1744] team could click on one creature, in a crowd scene of twenty thousand, and see through his "eyes". Different species even boast unique fighting styles.
  • [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977] joined the film when it was already shooting, never having met [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] before, nor indeed having read the J.R.R. Tolkien books. It was [Mortensen's][/p?id=1977] eleven-year-old son [Henry Mortensen][/p?id=Henry%20Mortensen], who was the chief instigator in convincing [Mortensen][/p?id=1977] to sign on as Aragorn.
  • Originally, the narration at the prologue was to be spoken by [Elijah Wood][/p?id=1272], but it was felt that the information imparted had little bearing on the character of Frodo. Sir [Ian McKellen][/p?id=1387] also recorded a narration, but once again, it was felt that Gandalf wasn't the right character to speak it. They eventually settled on [Cate Blanchett][/p?id=1137] as Galadriel, as it emphasizes the timelessness of the elves.
  • Veteran Sword Master [Bob Anderson][/p?id=Bob%20Anderson] called [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977] "the best swordsman I've ever trained".
  • [Peter Jackson's][/p?id=1744] original plan was to exclusively hire British actors for the roles of the hobbits. As it turned out, [Billy Boyd][/p?id=Billy%20Boyd] and [Dominic Monaghan][/p?id=Dominic%20Monaghan] were the only ones, and one of the tasks he charged them with was to coach [Elijah Wood][/p?id=1272] and [Sean Astin][/p?id=1864] in the ways of British pub culture.
  • [Orlando Bloom][/p?id=1710] landed the role of Legolas two days before he finished drama school.
  • [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977] chipped a tooth while filming a fight sequence. He wanted [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] to superglue it back on, so he could finish his scene, but [Jackson][/p?id=1744] took him to the dentist on his lunch break, had it patched up, and returned to the set that afternoon.
  • During filming, most of the members of the Fellowship took up surfing in New Zealand in their spare time. Among them was [Viggo Mortensen][/p?id=1977], who wiped out terribly one day, and bruised one whole side of his face. The next day, Make-up Artists tried to mask the bruising and swelling, but were unsuccessful. Instead, [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] opted to film Mortenson from one side for the entire scene. In the Mines of Moria, when they find the tomb, Aragorn is only seen from one side in the whole scene.
  • When the dragon firework goes off at the party, the shriek heard is [Billy Boyd][/p?id=Billy%20Boyd] actually screaming, as he was unaware at the time that the firework was really going to explode on-set (he thought that it would be in a separate shot). It was not scripted, but the take ended up in the final film.

  • The two most renowned Tolkien artists are [Alan Lee][/p?id=Alan%20Lee] and John Howe, and so it was important to [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] to have those two on board. [Lee][/p?id=1169] was tracked down to a tiny little village in Dartmoor, England, and was FedExed a package of [Jackson's][/p?id=1744] Heavenly Creatures (1994) and a letter outlining his intentions. They monitored the progress of the FedEx package every step of the way, but were somewhat surprised when [Lee][/p?id=1169] rang them only three hours after delivery, to say he'd love to work with them. Howe, meanwhile, was living in Switzerland, and because someone hadn't worked out the time differences between Switzerland and New Zealand correctly, he was called at about 2 a.m. He says that the biggest frustration with that phone call, was waiting for [Jackson][/p?id=1744] to finish his pitch, before he could say yes.

[Holm][/p?id=1386] was so delighted with the puppet, that the design team had a cast iron version of it made for his mantelpiece, and gave it to him as a parting gift, when [Holm][/p?id=1386] wrapped all his scenes on the film.
  • In one take of the Bucklebury Ferry scene, [Elijah Wood][/p?id=1272] overshot his jump, and ended up in the river instead of on the ferry.
  • Throughout the trilogy, the color of Legolas' eyes change from blue to brown. This is because the contact lenses [Orlando Bloom][/p?id=1710] was wearing scratched his corneas, and could not be worn every day. In some of the shots, the post-production team digitally changed the color of his eyes.

  • In the film's first theatrical release, a story circulated that when Sam tells Frodo that he is now the farthest he has ever been from home, a car is visible driving by in the background (top-right corner of the screen). Arguments ensued. Some said it was smoke from a chimney, others said they saw the glint of sunlight reflected from the windshield of a fast-moving vehicle. In the version of the film released on DVD, there is definitely no car, only chimney smoke and a one-frame flash of light, that could conceivably be a car, but not in any sense that could be considered a goof. [Peter Jackson][/p?id=1744] says (in the commentary track on the Extended DVD) that he looked at every frame on a computer, and has never seen anything resembling a car, and claims that it's nonsense (and certainly the original sighting remains unconfirmed by IMDb goof spotters). In the documentary of the Extended DVD version, Editor [John Gilbert][/p?id=John%20Gilbert] says that there was a car in the background, but they thought no one would notice it. They got rid of it in the DVD version. In a subsequent magazine interview, [Jackson][/p?id=1744] agreed that the car was there.

  • [Johnny Vegas][/p?id=Johnny%20Vegas] auditioned for the role of Sam. He recalled, "I was dreadful. I'm in front of a bluescreen, they go, 'Imagine a spider' and there's me going, 'Oooh, Shelob! Shelob!' [Peter Jackson's][/p?id=1744] taking his glasses off and rubbing his eyes, saying: I flew from New Zealand for this."
  • [Nicolas Cage][/p?id=1696] turned down the role of Aragorn, due to family obligations.
  • [Patrick McGoohan][/p?id=Patrick%20McGoohan] and [Nigel Hawthorne][/p?id=Nigel%20Hawthorne] turned down the role of Gandalf, due to ill health.
  • [Ian McKellen][/p?id=1387] was the only Best Supporting Actor Oscar nominee that year that was from a Best Picture nominated film.
