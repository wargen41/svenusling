  • Walter Burns ([Cary Grant][/p?id=1134]) refers to some horrible fate suffered by the last person who crossed him: Archie Leach. Grant's real name was Archibald Leach and he ad-libbed the line. See also Arsenic and Old Lace (1944).
  • When Earl Williams tries to get out of the roll-top desk, Burns ([Cary Grant][/p?id=1134]) says: "Get back in there, you Mock Turtle". Grant played the Mock Turtle in Alice in Wonderland (1933).
  • The famous in-joke about [Ralph Bellamy's][/p?id=Ralph%20Bellamy] character ("He looks like that actor...[Ralph Bellamy][/p?id=Ralph%20Bellamy]!") was almost left on the cutting room floor: [Harry Cohn][/p?id=Harry%20Cohn], the studio head, saw the dailies and responded in fury at the impertinence, but he let [Howard Hawks][/p?id=1376] leave it in, and it has always been one of the biggest laughs in the film.
  • One of the first, if not the first, films to have characters talk over the lines of other characters, for a more realistic sound. Prior to this, movie characters completed their lines before the next lines were started.
  • [Rosalind Russell][/p?id=Rosalind%20Russell] thought, while shooting, that she didn't have as many good lines as [Cary Grant][/p?id=1134] had, so she hired an advertisement writer through her brother-in-law and had him write more clever lines for the dialog. Since [Howard Hawks][/p?id=1376] allowed for spontaneity and ad-libbing, he, and many of the cast and crew didn't notice it, but Grant knew she was up to something, leading him to greet her every morning: "What have you got today?"
  • [Jean Arthur][/p?id=Jean%20Arthur] was the first choice to play Hildy. Among the other actresses who also turned down the role were [Carole Lombard][/p?id=Carole%20Lombard], [Ginger Rogers][/p?id=Ginger%20Rogers], [Claudette Colbert][/p?id=Claudette%20Colbert] and [Irene Dunne][/p?id=1395].
  • Voted #10 in Total Film's 100 Greatest Movies Of All Time list (November 2005).
  • Premiere voted this movie as one of "The 50 Greatest Comedies Of All Time" in 2006.
  • The only music is in the first and last two minutes of the film.
  • The incident with Walter and Hildy hiding escaped killer Earl Williams in a desk in the city room was based on a real incident. Emile Gauvreau, the editor of the old New York City paper "The New York Evening Graphic", hid an escaped killer in the city room of the newspaper, interviewed him, wrote the story and waited until the paper was on the street before turning him over to the police.
  • The Front Page opened at the Times Square Theater on August 14, 1929 and ran for 276 performances.
