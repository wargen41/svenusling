  • The film marked the beginning of mega-deals for Hollywood stars. [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] and [William Holden][/p?id=2005] received $775,000 each, plus 20% of the overall profits, an unheard-of sum for that time. The final contract involved six companies and numbered twice the pages of the movie's script. The film, however, was a financial failure, with no profits to be shared in the end.
  • Because of the lead actor's vastly different political views--[John Wayne][/p?id=1504] was very conservative and [William Holden][/p?id=2005] was very liberal--some of the animosity shown on the screen was real, a result of personal conflicts. Following the wrap each actor vowed to never again work with the other.
  • During filming of the climactic battle scene, veteran stuntman [Fred Kennedy][/p?id=Fred%20Kennedy] executed a fall from a horse improperly, broke his neck and died. According to fellow stuntmen, [Kennedy][/p?id=Fred%20Kennedy] had broken his neck two years earlier, but it had healed. [Kennedy][/p?id=Fred%20Kennedy] had appeared in several films directed by [John Ford][/p?id=1483] and the director was greatly affected by [Kennedy's][/p?id=Fred%20Kennedy] death. After the incident [Ford][/p?id=1483] halted filming and immediately moved the production back to Hollywood. The film was scripted to end with the triumphant arrival of Marlowe's forces in Baton Rouge, but [Ford][/p?id=1483] "simply lost interest" after [Kennedy's][/p?id=Fred%20Kennedy] death. He ended the film with Marlowe's farewell to Hannah Hunter before crossing and blowing up the bridge.
  • The film is based on the true-life raid by Col. Benjamin Grierson, who, as shown in the movie, began his expedition, known as Grierson's Raid, from LeGrange, TN, in April of 1863.
  • Director [John Ford's][/p?id=1483] only feature film set during the Civil War, although he did direct a segment of [How the West Was Won][/f?id=20028] (1962) that was set during the Civil War.
  • [John Ford][/p?id=1483] made sure to pay the black extras in Louisiana and Mississippi the same pay as the white extras, raising a few eyebrows in the Southern communities.
  • Lukey's dialogue was originally written in a stereotypic "Negro" dialect that [Althea Gibson][/p?id=Althea%20Gibson] found offensive. She informed [John Ford][/p?id=1483] that she would not deliver her lines as written. Though [Ford][/p?id=1483] was notorious for his intolerance of actors' demands, he agreed to modify the script.
  • Because of racial segregation laws in Louisiana, [Althea Gibson][/p?id=Althea%20Gibson] would have been forced to stay in separate housing during the shoot, so all her scenes were shot in Hollywood, with doubles used in long shots filmed on location.
  • The producers originally wanted [Clark Gable][/p?id=1178] to play Col. Marlowe, with [James Stewart][/p?id=1425] as Maj. Kendall. However, it was felt that the younger [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] and [William Holden][/p?id=2005] would be better suited to the characters.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] viewed this as a film that would allow him to funnel some of the profits into his own pet project, [][/f?id=10114]The Alamo:::: (1960), which he was already in the process of casting and producing.
