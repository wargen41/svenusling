  • In 1967 [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] wrote to Democratic President [Lyndon Johnson][/p?id=Lyndon%20Johnson] requesting military assistance for his pro-war film about Vietnam. The Defense Department had previously helped other war films like [Sands of Iwo Jima][/f?id=16675] (1949) and [The Longest Day][/f?id=11974] (1962). Jack Valenti told the President, "[Wayne's][/p?id=1504] politics are wrong, but insofar as Vietnam is concerned, his views are right. If he made the picture he would be saying the things we want said." [Wayne][/p?id=1504] got enough help from the Defense Department to make this film, which became one of the most controversial movies of all time.
  • [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] character, Col. Mike Kirby, is based on the real-life Lauri Törni, who later on called himself [Larry Thorne][/p?id=Larry%20Thor]. He was a Finnish army captain who fought in the Second World War during the Winter War (1939-40) and Continuation War (1941-44) against the Soviet Union. He emigrated to the U.S. in the late 1940s and in 1954 joined the U.S. Army. In November 1963 he joined Special Forces unit A-734 in Vietnam and fought in the Mekong Delta. He disappeared during a mission in 1965 and was reported MIA (Missing In Action). His remains were found in 1999, and formally identified in 2003.
  • Possibly due to the film's extremely negative critical reactions, it's been a long-held belief by many people that it was also a box-office flop. Actually, it was one of [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] biggest box-office successes, attracting millions of moviegoers and ending up being the 13th highest grossing movie of 1968, with reported grosses of 21,707,027 dollars.
  • At the beginning of filming [George Takei][/p?id=1330] told [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] he was strongly against the Vietnam War. [Wayne][/p?id=1504] replied that so was half of the cast and crew, and that he had hired [Takei][/p?id=1330] for his acting ability and not for his political views.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] said he believed the extremely negative reviews probably helped the film's box-office performance. He further said that he felt critics were attacking the war itself rather than his film.
  • When the movie was released several critics pointed out that Green Berets were not usually 60 years old.
  • [George Takei][/p?id=1330] missed nine episodes of [Star Trek][/f?id=19154] (1966) to work on this movie.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] traveled to Vietnam in June 1966 and got the idea to make a film about the Army Special Forces on that trip.
  • [Aldo Ray's][/p?id=Aldo%20Ray] alcoholism was a continual problem during filming, to the extent that [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] had to give some of [Ray's][/p?id=Aldo%20Ray] dialogue to other characters.
  • Producer Jack L. Warner, a hawk on the Vietnam War, remained publicly proud of the film despite the poor reviews.
  • [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] final war film, although [The Undefeated][/f?id=De%20Obesegrade] (1969) and [Rio Lobo][/f?id=17710] (1970) contained some war scenes.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] wanted his friend Elmer Bernstein to score the film but Bernstein felt compelled to turn the opportunity down, as he didn't feel it sat well with his politics.
  • [Luke Askew][/p?id=Luke%20Askew] replaced [Bruce Dern][/p?id=1114].
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] later said after this film was released, "The left-wingers are shredding my flesh, but like Liberace, we're bawling all the way to the bank."
  • It has been claimed that [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] turned down [Lee Marvin's][/p?id=1572] role in [The Dirty Dozen][/f?id=12%20F%C3%B6rd%C3%B6mda%20M%C3%A4n] (1967) in order to make this film. However, [The Dirty Dozen][/f?id=12%20F%C3%B6rd%C3%B6mda%20M%C3%A4n] (1967) was filmed early in 1966, whereas this film was made in the second half of 1967. Other sources say [Wayne][/p?id=1504] turned down [The Dirty Dozen][/f?id=12%20F%C3%B6rd%C3%B6mda%20M%C3%A4n] (1967) because he didn't want to be making a movie in the UK when his wife Pilar was due to give birth in February 1966.
  • Warner Bros. was concerned about letting [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] direct the movie because of the fact that his previous directorial effort, [The Alamo][/f?id=10114] (1960), had been an expensive flop. Therefore, they only agreed to let him do the film if he agreed to co-direct with a more experienced director, and [Wayne][/p?id=1504] chose [Ray Kellogg][/p?id=Ray%20Kellogg]. The studio agreed, despite [Ray][/p?id=Ray] Kellogg||Kellogg's** only having ever directed a few "B" pictures, because of his impressive track record as a second unit director on a number of major studio releases.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] was determined to have "The Ballad of the Green Berets" over the opening credits, even though others involved in the production of the film felt it was too corny and old-fashioned.
  • [Jim Hutton][/p?id=Jim%20Hutton] was against the Vietnam War, although his involvement in this film caused many to mistakenly believe he shared [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] pro-war views.
  • Late in the movie [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] can be seen to wrap his rappelling rope through a carabineer the wrong way. Called a "fatal hookup" in the Army, this would result in an immediate fall once weight was applied.
  • While there was much criticism of [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] age, it should be noted that most of the actors were considered to be too old to play soldiers. The average age of a U.S. soldier in Vietnam was 19.
  • Like his film character of George Beckworth, [David Janssen][/p?id=David%20Janssen] was against the Vietnam War and did not believe his character would have changed his mind to support U.S. involvement in it.
  • [George Takei][/p?id=1330] has admitted in interviews that while he was grateful to be cast in this film, he nevertheless strongly disagreed with the film's pro-war message and felt the finished movie was very bad.
  • [Oliver Stone][/p?id=1705] wrote [Platoon][/f?id=17462] (1986) partially as an attempt to redress what he considered to be some of the more reactionary elements of this movie.
  • The film bore little resemblance to the novel on which it was based.
  • This is one of many films to which Roger Ebert gave zero stars.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] wished the screenplay to have more development of the characters but Warner Bros. made it clear they wanted more action than talk as [AlamoThe Alamo][/f?id=AlamoThe%20Alamo] (1960) was heavily criticized for too much dialogue.
  • [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] and [Aldo Ray][/p?id=Aldo%20Ray] did not get along during filming. [Ray][/p?id=Aldo%20Ray] later spoke disparagingly of [Wayne][/p?id=1504] in interviews.
  • [George Takei][/p?id=1330] accepted his role in the film while on hiatus from shooting the first and second seasons of [Star Trek][/f?id=19154] (1966). As [Takei][/p?id=1330] would later note in several interviews, while he was on set, he received several of early scripts for [Star Trek][/f?id=19154] (1966) and was pleased to learn that his character, Mister Sulu, was going to get a more expanded role on the show. Several episodes were going to better feature him on landing missions and even allow his character some romantic plot lines with female co-stars. However, when the shooting of the film ran over schedule, clashing with the start of [Star Trek][/f?id=19154] (1966)'s filming, [Takei][/p?id=1330] had to bow out of the proposed episodes so the producers of the series created the character, Mister Checkov, to replace Sulu until [Takei][/p?id=1330] was available to return.
