Visste du?

  • [Duke Ellington][/p?id=Duke%20Ellington] did the music and has a cameo as "Pie-Eye."
  • The part of the judge was offered to both [Spencer Tracy][/p?id=1888] and [Burl Ives][/p?id=Burl%20Ives], but instead went to [Joseph N. Welch][/p?id=Joseph%20N.%20Welch] who was a lawyer in real life who had represented the U.S. Army in the televised Army-McCarthy hearings in 1954.
  • The part played by [Lee Remick][/p?id=1573] was first offered to [Lana Turner][/p?id=Lana%20Turner], who agreed to take it on the condition that she would wear gowns designed exclusively by her personal couturier, [Jean Louis][/p?id=Jean%20Louis]. When director [Otto Preminger][/p?id=1713] objected that such gowns were not suitable for the role, [Turner][/p?id=Lana%20Turner] turned down the part. Columbia was ready to give in to [Turner's][/p?id=Lana%20Turner] demands but [Preminger][/p?id=1713] resisted and gave the role to [Remick][/p?id=1573], then almost a beginner.
  • The [Lee Remick][/p?id=1573] part was turned down by [Jayne Mansfield][/p?id=Jayne%20Mansfield].
  • [Otto Preminger][/p?id=1713] originally wanted [Lee Remick][/p?id=1573] for the part of Laura because he had been impressed with her debut in A Face in the Crowd (1957) and knew that she could play a young sultry woman (even though [Remick][/p?id=1573] was 8 months pregnant when [Preminger][/p?id=1713] approached her for the role). A few weeks later he called to tell her that he had given the part to [Lana Turner][/p?id=Lana%20Turner] and instead offered her a smaller role of Mary Pilant, but [Remick][/p?id=1573] boldly refused. On an especially hectic day when [Remick][/p?id=1573] received a call saying that she had the part of Laura, she thought it was a joke and hung up. It took another phone call to convince her that she truly did have the lead female role.
  • The film was cut, scored and in previews only a month after filming had wrapped.
  • [James Stewart's][/p?id=1425] father was so offended by the film, which he deemed "a dirty picture", that he took out an ad in his local newspaper telling people not to see it.
  • Upon its original release, the film was banned in [Chicago][/f?id=11526], Illinois.
  • When [James Stewart's][/p?id=1425] character goes to the hotel to visit [Kathryn Grant][/p?id=Kathryn%20Grant], the clerk at the desk is reading Leon Uris's "[Exodus][/f?id=13522]". One year later, director [Preminger][/p?id=1713] would go on to direct its film version, [Exodus][/f?id=13522] (1960).
  • [Otto Preminger][/p?id=1713] disliked the use of flashbacks; hence there are none in the film.
