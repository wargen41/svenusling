Visste du?

  • [Frank Wills][/p?id=Frank%20Wills], the security guard who discovered the break-in at the Watergate complex, plays himself.
  • British director [John Schlesinger][/p?id=1498] declined an offer to direct as he felt the story of Watergate should be told by an American.
  • This was the first film [Jimmy Carter][/p?id=Jimmy%20Carter] watched during his presidential tenure.
  • Washington Post boss Katharine Graham, who was initially very apprehensive about the film using the paper's name, loved the film and later wrote a letter of praise and approval to star and co-producer [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821].
  • The telephone number that [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] dials for the White House is the real number of the White House Switchboard: 456-1414.
  • One scene involving [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] on the phone is done in a continuous six-minute single take with the camera tracking in slowly. Towards the end [Redford][/p?id=1821] makes a mistake - he calls the phone caller by the wrong name - but as he stays in character it simply appears genuine and this was the take used in the final cut.
  • When Kenneth Dahlberg tells Bob Woodward on the phone, "I've just been through a terrible ordeal! My neighbor's wife has been kidnapped!" he is not lying. On 27 July 1972, a few days before Woodward called Dahlberg, Virginia Piper - wife of a prominent Minnesota businessman and a close friend of the Dahlberg family - was kidnapped from her home in Minneapolis. She was released two days later in Duluth, after her husband paid a $1 million ransom.
  • On Tuesday 31 May 2005, in advance of a revelatory July 2005 "Vanity Fair" article written by his attorney and spokesman, ninety-one-year-old W. Mark Felt acknowledged publicly for the first time that he was in fact "Deep Throat," a fact corroborated by Bob Woodward and the Washington Post. At the time of the Watergate break-in, Mr. Felt was second in command at the F.B.I.
  • [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] bought the rights to Woodward and Bernstein's book in 1974 for $450,000.
  • [Dustin Hoffman][/p?id=1254] and [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] hung out in the Washington Post offices for months, sitting in on news conferences.
  • Nothing was allowed into the script unless it had been meticulously verified and confirmed by independent sources.
  • Warner Brothers agreed to finance the film only on condition that [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] - then the number one box office star - starred as Bob Woodward.
  • [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] felt that by casting him as Woodward he was unnecessarily unbalancing the film. The obvious answer was to cast a star of equal weight. For that reason, he approached [Dustin Hoffman][/p?id=1254] at a Knicks game and offered him the role of Bernstein.
  • 1976's second biggest hit after [One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest][/f?id=G%C3%B6kboet] (1975), outgrossing such films as The Bad News Bears (1976) and [The Omen][/f?id=17263] (1976).
  • [Jason Robards][/p?id=1437] was always [Robert Redford's][/p?id=1821] first choice to play Ben Bradlee. When director [Alan J. Pakula][/p?id=Alan%20J.%20Pakula] came on board, he instantly agreed with [Redford's][/p?id=1821] decision.
  • Jane [Alexander's][/f?id=10123] Oscar-nominated performance amounts to little more than 8 minutes screentime.
  • The film reunites [Martin Balsam][/p?id=1617] and [Jack Warden][/p?id=1404], who previously appeared together as jurors in [12 Angry Men][/f?id=20686] (1957) (coincidentally, sitting at ends of the table directly opposite one another.)
  • To ensure that both stars of the movie received top billing, [Robert Redford's][/p?id=1821] name was billed above [Dustin Hoffman's][/p?id=1254] on the posters and trailers, while [Hoffman's][/p?id=1254] name was billed above [Redford's][/p?id=1821] in the movie itself. This same strategy had been used for [The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance][/f?id=16024] (1962), which paired [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] and [James Stewart][/p?id=1425].
