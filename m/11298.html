  • Several of the major battle scenes had to be re-shot, as extras were seen wearing sunglasses and wristwatches.
  • Director/producer [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] was investigated by the RSPCA, who were convinced that the fake horses used were real. Only when one of his assistants provided some videotaped footage of the location shooting were they convinced otherwise.
  • When asked by a local why the Battle of Stirling Bridge was filmed on an open plain, [Gibson][/p?id=1635] answered that "the bridge got in the way". "Aye," the local answered. "That's what the English found."
  • The extras used for the battle scenes were mostly members of the F.C.A., the Irish version of the territorial army. As they were drawn from many different army companies, and the members of these are usually drawn from the same locality, local rivalry between such companies is common. Apparently, some of the battle scenes seen in the movie are far more realistic than you might imagine, with rival companies actually using the occasion to try the beat the lard out of each other.
  • [Wallace's][/p?id=William%20Wallace] real wife was named Marian, but it was changed to Murron to avoid confusion with the [Robin Hood][/f?id=Robin%20Hood] character of the same name.
  • [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] was supposed to star in [Terry Gilliam's][/p?id=1926] (never made) film "A Tale of Two Cities" but turned it down to star in this movie which he then offered to [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] to direct, but [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] declined.
  • Real life [Wallaces][/p?id=William%20Wallace] are extras in the movie. [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] also stayed with them during the course of the film to learn history.
  • The battle of Stirling took six weeks to film; roughly half a million feet of film (90+ hours) were shot for the sequence.
  • Randall [Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace] had very little historical evidence to work with in regard to [William Wallace's][/p?id=William%20Wallace] life; he has noted that even Churchill's definitive work "A History of the English Speaking Peoples" observed in only a single line that virtually no factual material survives about the Scottish leader. Because of this, Randall [Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace] relied heavily on a 15th-century romantic poem by the Scottish writer Henry the Minstrel ("Blind Harry") in constructing his story.
  • Writer Randall [Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace] initially planned to start the story with [William Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace] as an adult and added the prologue of his childhood only as an afterthought. As the sequence was first written, Murran gave William a rose (rather than a thistle) at his father's burial; however, someone who read the script helpfully pointed out that the rose, being a traditional symbol of England, would be (to say the least) somewhat inappropriate as a prominent feature in the story.
  • Single frames of film were removed at strategic points in the battles in order to produce a jarring, startling effect.
  • The only way [Gibson][/p?id=1635] could get the film made was if he agreed with Paramount studios that he would star in the film as well.
  • Blue body paint for battles had stopped being used around the end of the Roman era - roughly 800 years before the events of the film.
  • [Brian Cox][/p?id=1104] who plays Argyle [Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace] was first offered a larger role but took the role of Argyle because he felt it was a better role.
  • [Gibson][/p?id=1635], a notorious jokester, directed some scenes in an Elmer Fudd voice and even yelled, "CUT!" during Murron's funeral scene by putting his arm around the actress playing her mother and hollering, "Will you put a sock in it!" This caused the actress to go from crying in character to break character and laugh. [Gibson][/p?id=1635] also intentionally started a false rumor that [Sophie Marceau][/p?id=Sophie%20Marceau] was the daughter of noted French mime [Marcel Marceau][/p?id=Marcel%20Marceau].
  • [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] originally wanted [Jason Patric][/p?id=Jason%20Patric] to play [William Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace].
  • Its Oscar win was the only award it won for "Best Picture" (no other award or critic group named it the best film of the year).
  • [Sean Connery][/p?id=1866] turned down the role of King Edward I because he was filming [Just Cause][/f?id=15004] (1995).
  • Many Scots were offended by the film's portrayal of Robert the Bruce.
  • [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] initially turned down the role of [William Wallace][/p?id=William%20Wallace], because he felt he was too old for the part.
  • [Mel Gibson][/p?id=1635] has said he would give five dollars to anyone who could spot the fake horses in the final film. Reportedly he has not had to make good on the wager.
