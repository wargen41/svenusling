  • [Frank Capra's][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] first cut of the film ran for 6 hours. The first public preview took place in Santa Barbara when the film ran for 3 1/2 hours. Re-shooting and re-cutting followed immediately after this disastrous preview.
  • [John Howard][/p?id=John%20Howard] was tested for his part two days before production began. [David Niven][/p?id=1221] and Louis Hayward had both already tested for the role.
  • Studio head [Harry Cohn][/p?id=Harry%20Cohn] didn't like [Sam Jaffe's][/p?id=Sam%20Jaffe] performance as the High Lama and insisted that [Capra][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] shoot it with another actor. [Capra][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] had to submit to this request and a test with [Walter Connolly][/p?id=Walter%20Connolly] was made, with [Cohn][/p?id=Harry%20Cohn] even insisting on an expensive new set being built specially for it. Despite loading the dice in [Connolly's][/p?id=Walter%20Connolly] favor, the consensus was that his test wasn't anywhere near as good. So [Jaffe][/p?id=Sam%20Jaffe] won the part back, though he still had to re-shoot all his scenes as they were deemed to be far too lengthy and wordy.
  • Originally there was an opening prologue in which a weary [Ronald Colman][/p?id=Ronald%20Colman] on a cruise ship is prompted to tell his amazing story of the land of Shangri-La. Although alluded to in the closing passages of the film, no footage of this prologue has ever been found. [Capra][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] claims he burned it.
  • After initial reservations about [Frank Capra's][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] method of directing, [Ronald Colman][/p?id=Ronald%20Colman] would eventually come to rely on him and the two would experiment with improvisations.
  • [Frank Capra][/p?id=Frank%20Capra] hated screen tests; scripts were developed with specific actors in mind. [Ronald Colman][/p?id=Ronald%20Colman] was first choice to play Conway from the very beginning. It was only over who should play the High Lama that he had problems and had to resort to screen tests.
  • Filmed in 1936, not released until 1937.
  • The Lamasery set was, at the time, the largest single standing set in terms of square feet built for a motion picture of the sound era.
