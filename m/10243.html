  • Producer-Director [Robert Rossen][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] offered the role of Willie Stark to [John Wayne][/p?id=1504]. [Rossen][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] sent a copy of the script to [Wayne's][/p?id=1504] agent, Charles K. Feldman, who forwarded it to [Wayne][/p?id=1504]. After reading the script, [Wayne][/p?id=1504] sent it back with an angry letter attached. In it, he told Feldman that before he sent the script to any of his other clients, he should ask them if they wanted to star in a film that "smears the machinery of government for no purpose of humor or enlightenment," that "degrades all relationships," and that is populated by "drunken mothers; conniving fathers; double-crossing sweethearts; bad, bad, rich people; and bad, bad poor people if they want to get ahead." He accused [Rossen][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] of wanting to make a movie that threw acid on "the American way of life." If Feldman had such clients, [Wayne][/p?id=1504] wrote that the agent should "rush this script... to them." [Wayne][/p?id=1504], however, said to the agent that "You can take this script and shove it up [Robert Rossen's][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] derri?re..." [Wayne][/p?id=1504] later remarked that "To make Huey Long a wonderful, rough pirate was great," he said; "but, according to this picture, everybody was s - t except for this weakling intern doctor who was trying to find a place in the world." [Broderick Crawford][/p?id=Broderick%20Crawford], who had played a supporting role in [Wayne's][/p?id=1504] [Seven Sinners][/f?id=18480] (1940), eventually received the part of Stark. In a bit of irony, [Crawford][/p?id=Broderick%20Crawford] was Oscar-nominated for the part of Stark and found himself competing against [Wayne][/p?id=1504], who was nominated the same year for Sands of Iwo Jima (1949). [Crawford][/p?id=Broderick%20Crawford] won the Best Actor Oscar, giving [Rossen][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] the last laugh.
  • [Mercedes McCambridge's][/p?id=Mercedes%20McCambridge] film debut.
  • [Mercedes McCambridge][/p?id=Mercedes%20McCambridge] was cast after she got angry with the producers. She and other actresses were kept waiting in an office in New York City during open auditions. [McCambridge][/p?id=Mercedes%20McCambridge] told the producers off and stormed out of the office. They called her back and eventually cast her because she fit the part of Sadie.
  • Residents of the area around Stockton, California were used as extras in the film; often, director [Robert Rossen][/p?id=Robert%20Rossen] would give them speaking parts and film the "rehearsals" to get a more spontaneous effect.
  • To prepare for the role of Willie Stark, [Broderick Crawford][/p?id=Broderick%20Crawford] watched newsreels of Huey Long, the former Louisiana senator and governor on whose life the novel was loosely based.
