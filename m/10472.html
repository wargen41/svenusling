  • The role of Jack the dog was actually played by three matching Jack Russell Terriers: Uggie; Dash; and Dude, although The lead dog Uggie did the majority of scenes. All three dogs were colored before the filming began, made to look more alike.
  • [Penelope Ann Miller][/p?id=Penelope%20Ann%20Miller] also played [Edna Purviance][/p?id=Edna%20Purviance], a famous silent movie actress, in the 1992 movie [Chaplin][/f?id=11492], the bio-film about one of the most famous and renowned silent film comedians, [Charles Chaplin][/p?id=1145].
  • Peppy's house in the film is Mary Pickford's house, and the bed where George Valentin wakes up is Mary Pickford's bed.
  • During filming, [Jean Dujardin][/p?id=Jean%20Dujardin] lived in an isolated 1930s house in the Hollywood Hills.
  • In solitude, George views a reel from one of his silent swashbucklers through a film projector centered within his apartment. The film is in fact a genuine silent film, The Mark of Zorro, which established its star, [Douglas Fairbanks][/p?id=Douglas%20Fairbanks], as a real life silent era action hero and matin√©e idol, the kind George Valentin is portrayed as being within the film. The scene from Zorro is altered, however, substituting actor [Jean Dujardin][/p?id=Jean%20Dujardin] as George for [Fairbanks][/p?id=Douglas%20Fairbanks] for the close-up shots.

  • First completely black-and-white film to win the Best Picture Academy Award since [Billy Wilder's][/p?id=1088] [The Apartment][/f?id=K%C3%A4rleksn%C3%A4stet] just over half a century earlier. [Wilder][/p?id=1088] was actually thanked three times during the Best Picture Oscar acceptance speech for this movie. The film is also the first black-and-white film to win this award since the predominantly black-and-white [Schindler's List][/f?id=18009] eighteen years earlier.
  • [Jean Dujardin][/p?id=Jean%20Dujardin] became the first ever French actor to win a Best Actor Academy Award when he won an Oscar for this film.
  • This movie is considered to be the most ever awarded French Film in film history.
  • This film was one of a group of films that were in competition at the 2012 Academy Awards that referenced film history. This film and [The Artist][/f?id=10472], which both won five Academy Awards, examined silent cinema; [The Help][/f?id=17076] referenced [Gone with the Wind][/f?id=11270], its Best Supporting Actress winner [Octavia Spencer][/p?id=1700] evoking [Hattie McDaniel][/p?id=Hattie%20McDaniel] from that classic; whilst [My Week With Marilyn][/f?id=My%20Week%20With%20Marilyn] with two nominations was about the making of [The Prince and the Showgirl][/f?id=17523]
  • This film was inspired by the work of film directors such as [Fritz Lang][/p?id=1308], [Alfred Hitchcock][/p?id=1021] [John Ford][/p?id=1483], [Ernst Lubitsch][/p?id=Ernst%20Lubitsch], F.W. Murnau and [Billy Wilder][/p?id=1088].
  • The Weinstein's second consecutive Best Picture winner; the previous year's Best Picture Winner was [The King's Speech][/f?id=15498].
  • The breakfast montage in this movie, showing the breakdown of the marriage is a direct tribute to an almost identical montage in [Orson Welles'][/p?id=1711] [Citizen Kane][/f?id=11554].
  • The film's win for the Academy Award for Best Picture, the year after [The King's Speech][/f?id=15498] marked the first time since 1981-82 that there were two consecutive Best Picture winners which were produced outside the United States (from UK/Australia and France/Belgium, respectively). The first time was with [Chariots of Fire][/f?id=20764] and [Gandhi][/f?id=Gandhi] (both were British productions).
