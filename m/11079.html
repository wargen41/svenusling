  • [David S. Ward][/p?id=David%20S.%20Ward] got the idea for this movie when he was working on the script for [Steelyard Blues][/f?id=Operation%20Helkn%C3%A4pp] (1973), which includes a pickpocketing scene. Researching this, Ward found himself reading about con artists. Ward had shown the other screenplay to [Tony Bill][/p?id=Tony%20Bill], so he now gave him an outline of this story. [Bill][/p?id=Tony%20Bill] liked it immediately and brought in Julia Phillips[ and ][/p?id=%20and%20]Michael Phillips[; the three then produced both films. Ward wrote the script with ][/p?id=%3B%20the%20three%20then%20produced%20both%20films.%20Ward%20wrote%20the%20script%20with%20]Robert Redford[ in mind as Hooker, but ][/p?id=%20in%20mind%20as%20Hooker%2C%20but%20]Robert Redford||Redford[ initially turned the part down. Even after changing his mind, he didn't expect the movie to be a hit. ][/p?id=%20initially%20turned%20the%20part%20down.%20Even%20after%20changing%20his%20mind%2C%20he%20didn't%20expect%20the%20movie%20to%20be%20a%20hit.%20]Robert Shaw[ got the part of Lonnegan only after ][/p?id=%20got%20the%20part%20of%20Lonnegan%20only%20after%20]Richard Boone[ and another actor had declined it. ][/p?id=%20and%20another%20actor%20had%20declined%20it.%20]George Roy Hill[ saw the screenplay by accident and asked for the director's job. He routinely showed his projects to ][/p?id=%20saw%20the%20screenplay%20by%20accident%20and%20asked%20for%20the%20director's%20job.%20He%20routinely%20showed%20his%20projects%20to%20]Paul Newman[, and ][/p?id=%2C%20and%20]Paul Newman||Newman** was pleased to join this one. Hill wanted to film the picture on location, but Henry Bumstead was adamant that it would be much too hard to get the period appearance right; for example, things like lane markings on the streets. In the end, the only location shooting was a few days' worth in [Chicago][/f?id=11526] and Los Angeles; most of the exteriors were filmed on Universal's back lot.
  • According to costume designer Edith Head's biography, [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] and [Paul Newman][/p?id=1727], both of whom have blue eyes, wanted their shirts to be blue in order to emphasize their eyes. As a compromise, Head outfitted each man in blue in alternating scenes. Unfortunately, although it is an attractive story, it's a complete myth. A simple viewing of the film reveals the truth. [Newman][/p?id=1727] is never outfitted in blue in the whole film. He is first seen in a white vest in the brothel scenes. On the train he alternates between a brown striped shirt and a white one, which may or may not be a continuity error. From then until the end of the film, he is seen exclusively in a dinner jacket and white shirt. [Redford][/p?id=1821] wears a blue shirt on a couple of occasions, but even this doesn't really fit with this oddly persistent legend.
  • [Robert Redford's][/p?id=1821] character (Johnny Hooker) is supposedly named after blues legend [John Lee Hooker][/p?id=John%20Lee%20Hooker].
  • [Jack Nicholson][/p?id=1402] turned down the role of Johnny Hooker before [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] changed his mind and decided to play it.
  • The movie was filmed on the backlot of Universal studios and the diner in which Hooker meets Lonnegan is the same diner interior used in [Back to the Future][/f?id=20376] (1985) in which Marty McFly first meets his father and calls Doc Brown.
  • First Universal Studios film to win the Best Picture Oscar since [All Quiet on the Western Front][/f?id=17571] (1930).
  • [Robert Redford][/p?id=1821] didn't see the movie until June 2004.
  • [Richard Boone][/p?id=Richard%20Boone] was the first choice for the role of Lonnegan. [Oliver Reed][/p?id=1704] was offered the role but refused to test for it (he would later play Lonnegan in the sequel). [Stephen Boyd][/p?id=Stephen%20Boyd] was also considered.
  • Edith Head won her 8th and final Best Costume Design Academy Award for this film.
