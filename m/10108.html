  • Animator [Eric Goldberg][/p?id=Eric%20Goldberg] based the Genie on the drawings of caricaturist Al Hirschfeld.
  • The opening scene with the street merchant was completely unscripted. [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] was brought into the sound stage and was asked to stand behind a table that had several objects on it and a bed sheet covering them all. The animators asked him to lift the sheet, and without looking take an object from the table and describe it in character. Much of the material in that recording session was not appropriate for a Disney film.
  • Some of the Genie's imitations were cut from the film, including [John Wayne][/p?id=1504], [George Bush][/p?id=George%20Bush] and Dr. Ruth Westheimer.
  • [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] provided the voice for the Genie, at union scale rate (the lowest legal pay rate a studio can give an actor), on the provisos that his voice was not used for merchandising (i.e. toys and such) and that the Genie character not take up more than 25% of the space of a poster, ad, billboard, or trailer. When these wishes were not granted, he withdrew his support for Disney and the film. As a result, his name was not included in "The Art of [Aladdin][/f?id=Aladdin]" book (it makes constant references to "the voice of the Genie"), and he was not available for the direct-to-video sequel [The Return of Jafar][/f?id=15188] (1994) (V) or the "[Aladdin][/f?id=Aladdin]" (1994) TV show ([Dan Castellaneta][/p?id=1197] filled in as the voice of the Genie for these productions). In an attempt to get back on good terms with [Williams][/p?id=1828], Walt Disney Co. CEO Michael Eisner apologized to him with a peace offering of an original Pablo Picasso painting. Still angered and feeling betrayed by Disney, [Williams][/p?id=1828] would not accept the gift. It was not until Jeffrey Katzenberg was fired and a new producer hired did [Williams][/p?id=1828] return to Disney. Through this new producer, a public apology was given (by the producer). Promises were made to right wrongs, and [Williams][/p?id=1828] was so touched that he came back as the Genie for the second direct-to-video sequel, [Aladdin and the King of Thieves][/f?id=10112] (1995) (V). Disney was so thrilled that they threw out the previously completed recording sessions with [Castellaneta][/p?id=1197].
  • This film became the 14th (and the first animated movie) to gross more than $200,000,000.
  • The most successful film of 1992, earning over $217 million at the US box office and over $504 million worldwide.
  • This was the first major animated film which was advertised on the strength of having a major movie star providing one of its voices ([Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] in this case). This has since become the norm with animated features.
  • [John Candy][/p?id=1475], [Steve Martin][/p?id=1903] and [Eddie Murphy][/p?id=1261] were all considered at one point to provide the voice of the Genie.
  • [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] recorded most of his scenes in between filming breaks on [Hook][/f?id=14623] (1991) and [Toys][/f?id=20711] (1992).
  • In early visual development, [Aladdin][/f?id=Aladdin] resembled [Michael J. Fox][/p?id=1652]. As the film developed, Jeffrey Katzenberg didn't think [Aladdin][/f?id=Aladdin] had enough appeal to women, so he asked that [Aladdin][/f?id=Aladdin] be beefed up a bit to resemble [Tom Cruise][/p?id=1949].
  • Jasmine's appearance was influenced by [Jennifer Connelly][/p?id=1447], as well as the sister of her animator, Mark Henn.
  • During the course of recording the voices, [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] improvised so much they had almost 16 hours of material.
  • While filming this movie, [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] frequently received calls from [Steven Spielberg][/p?id=1907] who at the time was working on [Schindler's List][/f?id=18009] (1993). He would put him on speaker phone so he could tell jokes to the cast and crew to cheer them up. Some of the material that he used was material that he was using for this film.
  • Scheduling conflicts with "[Star Trek: The Next Generation][/f?id=19461]" (1987) forced [Patrick Stewart][/p?id=1717] to turn down the role of Jafar. He has said in interviews that this is his biggest regret.
  • Because [Robin Williams][/p?id=1828] ad-libbed so many of his lines, the script was turned down for a Best Adapted Screenplay Academy Award nomination.
