  • [Chuck Norris][/p?id=1174] was originally cast to play the title role in the film.
  • [Michael Dudikoff][/p?id=1647] knew no martial arts before filming began.
  • Body count: 114
  • [Steve James][/p?id=Steve%20James] said in an interview that [Michael Dudikoff][/p?id=1647] had much anxiety working with him, for fear of being shown up as a result of a lesser knowledge of martial arts than his sidekick. [James][/p?id=Steve%20James] even requested his final fight scenes be filmed on the other side of the compound, far away from [Dudikoff][/p?id=1647], to avoid any further problems. It wasn't until halfway through filming the sequel that the two of them set their differences aside.
  • Director [Sam Firstenberg][/p?id=Sam%20Firstenberg] has stated in an interview that [American Ninja][/f?id=10304] (along with Breakin' 2: Electric Boogaloo (1984)) is one of the two favorite films he directed. He says [American Ninja][/f?id=10304] "has a special quality of innocent, true friendship, love and youthful idealism."
  • Director [Sam Firstenberg][/p?id=Sam%20Firstenberg] admitted in an interview that audiences at the time didn't like seeing a women ninja in the film Ninja III: The Domination (1984). This is why Cannon films sought to find a "[James Dean][/p?id=James%20Dean]"-like action star to be the ninja in [American Ninja][/f?id=10304]. They wanted someone that looked like [James Dean][/p?id=James%20Dean]; didn't talk much and seemed to carry a chip on their shoulder. During this process Sam auditioned 2000 young men and narrowed it down to 200. Though [Michael Dudikoff][/p?id=1647] had no martial arts training, he had the look Cannon was looking for. Hence he was cast in the lead.
  • In an interview in the late eighties, [Steve James][/p?id=Steve%20James] stated that when he came on board [American Ninja][/f?id=10304] he wanted to change a lot of the dialogue, as it was written like "no problems, whatever you say....partner."
