  • [Richard Gere][/p?id=1787] really is an accomplished trumpet player and performed his own solos in this movie.
  • The character of Dixie Dwyer is likely based on actor [George Raft][/p?id=George%20Raft]. [Raft][/p?id=George%20Raft] grew up dirt poor in New York's Hell's Kitchen. As a young man he worked as a driver/gopher for gangster Owney Madden (also depicted in this film). After his stint working for Madden he went to California and tried his hand at acting, eventually achieving considerable success playing tough-guy roles in various gangster movies, performances he based largely on Madden and Bugsy Siegel, with whom [Raft][/p?id=George%20Raft] was known to associate.
  • At one point relations were so strained between director [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] and producer [Robert Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] that Coppola had [Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] banned from the set.
  • When [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] called up [Bob Hoskins][/p?id=1091] to offer him a part, the actor didn't believe it was really him. Coppola introduced himself, to which [Hoskins][/p?id=1091] replied, "Yeah, and this is Henry the fucking Eighth", and hung up.
  • The relationship in the film between the characters played by [Gregory Hines][/p?id=Gregory%20Hines] and Maurice [Hines][/p?id=Gregory%20Hines] was partly based on their real lives.
  • [Laurence Fishburne's][/p?id=1567] character, Bumpy Rhodes, was based on real-life Harlem gangster Ellsworth "Bumpy" Johnson. [Fishburne][/p?id=1567] would play Bumpy in [Hoodlum][/f?id=Hoodlum] (1997) 13 years later.
  • According to producer [Robert Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans], [Richard Pryor][/p?id=1790] was originally cast as Sandman Williams but the budget could not accommodate his salary.
  • Although the film features several fictional murders, it is associated with one real-life murder. When [Robert Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] was raising money for the film, he became associated with a promoter named [Roy Radin][/p?id=Roy%20Radin]. [Radin][/p?id=Roy%20Radin] was reportedly murdered on May 13, 1983, but his body was not found for several weeks. [Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] was not accused of murder but was implicated in the investigation because he was dealing with [Radin][/p?id=Roy%20Radin] and Karen Jacobs-Greenberger, who introduced [Radin][/p?id=Roy%20Radin] to [Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans]. She was convicted of ordering the murder of [Radin][/p?id=Roy%20Radin] and is serving a life sentence for the crime.
  • Pulitzer-Prize winning novelist William Kennedy was brought in to do a quick draft on the script to polish it. He ended up doing over 20 drafts.
  • [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] made this film to pay off his debts from [One from the Heart][/f?id=%C3%84lskling%2C%20Jag%20Hatar%20Dig] (1981).
  • As depicted in the movie, Owney Madden and Big Frenchy DeMange were close friends and partners in the Cotton Club. Ironically, as younger men they belonged to rival crime syndicates and were bitter enemies.
  • Allegedly, the reconciliation scene between Owney Madden and Frenchy was written by [Fred Gwynne][/p?id=Fred%20Gwynne]. [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] considered removing the scene during editing but was convinced by co-screenwriter William Kennedy to leave it in.
  • [Sylvester Stallone][/p?id=1915] was the original choice for the lead, but backed out angrily when he found out producer [Robert Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] was having an affair with his then girlfriend.
  • The role of Vera Cicero was originally assigned to [Brooke Shields][/p?id=Brooke%20Shields]. However, she turned down the offer in order to study French Literature at Princeton University.
  • Enticed by a script by [Mario Puzo][/p?id=Mario%20Puzo] and the promise of funding from Las Vegas casino-owning brothers Edward Doumani and Fred Doumani, Paramount offered producer [Robert Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] the talents of [Richard Gere][/p?id=1787] as leading man and access to its studio facilities along with further production funds. However, determined to re-establish his reputation as a major player in Hollywood, [Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] turned down the latter offer in favor of the services of Orion Pictures--which was in the business of marketing and distributing films rather than producing them, meaning that [Evans][/p?id=Robert%20Evans] would need to raise more production money and find a studio in which to shoot the film, causing further delays and adding to the already bloated budget.
  • [Francis Ford Coppola's][/p?id=1299] quasi-improvisational approach to directing the actors meant the script was in a constant state of flux, and actors would frequently spend all day on set without shooting a single frame of film.
  • There were frequent clashes between [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] and [Richard Gere][/p?id=1787], who insisted on showing off his skills on the cornet in the film, and seemed more concerned about possible damage to his reputation than about the film itself.
  • Over 600 people built sets, created costumes and arranged music at a reported $250,000 a day.
  • According to [Gregory Hines][/p?id=Gregory%20Hines], a three-hour film was shot during rehearsals.
  • [Sigourney Weaver][/p?id=1882] turned down the role of Lila Rose Oliver.
  • [Al Pacino][/p?id=1008] turned down the lead role, feeling it was too close to [The Godfather][/f?id=14369] (1972). This was before [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] was brought in to direct.
  • [Francis Ford Coppola][/p?id=1299] considered [Matt Dillon][/p?id=Matt%20Dillon] for the lead role.
