Visste du?

  • Asked why, in the climactic chase scene, the Indians didn't simply shoot the horses to stop the stagecoach, director [John Ford][/p?id=1483] replied, "Because that would have been the end of the movie."
  • The first of many collaborations between [John Ford][/p?id=1483] and [John Wayne][/p?id=1504].
  • When the film was being cast, [John Ford][/p?id=1483] lobbied hard for [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] but producer Walter Wanger kept saying no. It was only after constant persistence on [Ford's][/p?id=1483] part that Wanger finally gave in. Wanger's reservations were based on [Wayne's][/p?id=1504] string of B-movies, in which he came across as being a less than competent actor, and the box office failure of Raoul Walsh's [The Big Trail][/f?id=Mot%20Lyckans%20Land] in 1930, [Wayne's][/p?id=1504] first serious starring role.
  • [John Wayne's][/p?id=1504] 80th film.
  • [John Ford][/p?id=1483] loved the Monument Valley location so much that the actual stagecoach journey traverses the valley three times.
  • David O. Selznick was interested in making the film, but only if he could have [Gary Cooper][/p?id=1314] as the Ringo Kid and [Marlene Dietrich][/p?id=Marlene%20Dietrich] as Dallas.
  • [John Ford's][/p?id=1483] first sound Western, and his first in that genre in 13 years. Westerns had fallen out favor with the coming of sound, as it was tricky to record on location.
  • [John Ford][/p?id=1483] gave [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] the script, asking him for any suggestions as to who could play the Ringo Kid. [Wayne][/p?id=1504] suggested [Lloyd Nolan][/p?id=Lloyd%20Nolan], not realizing that [Ford][/p?id=1483] was baiting him with the part. Once filming began, however, [Ford][/p?id=1483] was merciless to [Wayne][/p?id=1504], constantly undermining him. This psychological tactic was designed to make [Wayne][/p?id=1504] start feeling some real emotions, and not to be intimidated by acting alongside the likes of such seasoned professionals as [Thomas Mitchell][/p?id=1933].
  • In 1939 [Claire Trevor][/p?id=Claire%20Trevor] was the film's biggest star, and thus commanded the highest salary.
  • [Orson Welles][/p?id=1711] privately watched this film about 40 times while he was making [Citizen Kane][/f?id=11554] (1941).
  • Near the end of the movie, Luke Plummer ([Tom Tyler][/p?id=1954]) has a pair of black aces and a pair of black eights. This is the notorious "dead man's hand" supposed to have been held by Wild Bill Hickok before he was killed.
  • [John Ford][/p?id=1483] originally wanted [Ward Bond][/p?id=1989] to play Buck the stage driver but gave the role to [Andy Devine][/p?id=Andy%20Devine] when he found that [Bond][/p?id=1989] couldn't drive a "six-up" stagecoach and there wasn't time to teach him.
  • Producer Walter Wanger wanted [Gary Cooper][/p?id=1314] for the role of Ringo but [Cooper's][/p?id=1314] fees were too high. [Bruce Cabot][/p?id=Bruce%20Cabot] unsuccessfully tested for it before [John Ford][/p?id=1483] got his wish and cast [John Wayne][/p?id=1504].
  • The hat that [John Wayne][/p?id=1504] wears is his own. He would wear it in many westerns during the next two decades before retiring it after [Howard Hawks'][/p?id=1376] [Rio Bravo][/f?id=17708] (1959), because it was simply "falling apart." After that, the hat was displayed under glass in his home.
