Visste du?

  • The French entry in the race, flown by Pierre Dubois, is a replica of the "Demoiselle", designed by the [Brazilian][/f?id=11299] expatriate Alberto Santos Dumont, who had been believed by the French to be the first to fly a powered aircraft, until Wilbur and Orville Wright's demonstrations in 1908. The replica builders were faithful in constructing the Demoiselle, but no one could get it to leave the ground until it was discovered that Dumont had been a very small man who weighed only eighty-five pounds (thirty-eight and a half kilograms). Female pilot Joan Hughes was hired, and she successfully flew the plane throughout filming.
  • Most of the aerial scenes were filmed before ten a.m. each day when the air was least turbulent.
  • The 1910-era airplanes used in this movie were replicas built using the authentic materials of the originals, but with slightly more powerful engines. About twenty planes were built at a cost of about five thousand pounds sterling each.
  • [Michael Trubshawe's][/p?id=Michael%20Trubshawe] character's name is "[Niven][/p?id=1221]". [Michael Trubshawe][/p?id=Michael%20Trubshawe] and [David Niven][/p?id=1221] were very close friends from when they served in the military garrison at Malta together, and [David Niven][/p?id=1221] would call uncredited characters in his movies "[Trubshawe][/p?id=Michael%20Trubshawe]" - so this was [Trubshawe's][/p?id=Michael%20Trubshawe] way of returning the compliment.
  • The beginning and ending sequences mark this as the final theatrical movie appearence for [Red Skelton][/p?id=Red%20Skelton].
  • Writer, Producer, and Director [Ken Annakin][/p?id=Ken%20Annakin] had been working on an adventure movie about transatlantic flight when the producer's bankruptcy aborted the production. He then made this movie.
  • Ad art and credit sequences were designed by the noted satiric British military artist Ronald Searle.
  • At three hundred thousand dollars, this was [Stuart Whitman's][/p?id=1909] largest paycheck.
