  • [Joseph Cotten][/p?id=1512] would grumble on the set that he had to remember and deliver lines, while [Vincent Price's][/p?id=1978] were all to be post-dubbed. [Price][/p?id=1978] responded, "Yes, but I still know them, Joe." In fact, [Price][/p?id=1978] was well-known in Hollywood for his ability to memorize all of the characters' lines in a given production, not just his own.
  • [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] often cracked up during filming, wrecking his makeup.
  • [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] said [Joseph Cotten][/p?id=1512] was very uncomfortable doing his scenes, so he intentionally made a lot of funny faces to make him laugh.
  • For visual reasons, the plagues of flies and gnats were replaced with rats and bats.
  • In order to gain more publicity this film was advertised as [Vincent Price's][/p?id=1978] 100th feature film.
  • In the script Phibes was abusive to Vulnavia, eventually stabbing her to death, and then escape his house (which was to catch fire) in a hot air balloon with Victoria's body. It was decided to make Phibes a more sympathetic character, so these sequences were removed.
  • Set in 1925; the Phibes' tombstones say 1921, and the characters say Anton and Victoria died 4 years earlier.
  • The rat curse was originally intended to take place on a boat. Robert Fuest changed it to inside a plane, as he felt the boat could have been escaped from.
  • [Peter Cushing][/p?id=1738] was originally cast as Vesalius, but he declined because his wife was in poor health at the time.
  • Dr. Phibes' first words are spoken 32 minutes into the movie.
  • For the many scenes in which [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] was required to speak without moving his mouth, he was painted with a substance called collodion which literally immobilized his face.
  • In order for [Joseph Cotten][/p?id=1512] to know his cues, Phibes' dialogue was read aloud by a crew-member.
  • A young [Joanna Lumley][/p?id=Joanna%20Lumley] appeared as a laboratory assistant, but her scenes were cut.
  • [Virginia North][/p?id=Virginia%20North] appeared in a play in London called "Council of Love," in which she was silent throughout her performance. "I played the daughter of the devil," she told the Associated Press in 1971. "I'd go around and do terrible things. The people who made the movie saw me in the play and decided I was what they wanted. You might say I was ready made for the movie part."
  • To avoid spending hours in the makeup chair, [Vincent Price's][/p?id=1978] transformation was achieved by simply applying a rubber mask to the actor.
