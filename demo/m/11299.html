  • [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] tested more than half a dozen actors to play the part of Jill, interviewing or testing [Jamie Lee Curtis][/p?id=1428], [Rebecca De Mornay][/p?id=Rebecca%20De%20Mornay], [Rae Dawn Chong][/p?id=Rae%20Dawn%20Chong], [Joanna Pacula][/p?id=Joanna%20Pacula], [Rosanna Arquette][/p?id=Rosanna%20Arquette], [Kelly McGillis][/p?id=Kelly%20McGillis], [Ellen Barkin][/p?id=Ellen%20Barkin], and he even considered Madonna. [Gilliam's][/p?id=1926] personal favorite was [Barkin][/p?id=Ellen%20Barkin].
  • [Robert De Niro][/p?id=1808] wanted to play the role of Jack, but [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] had already promised this to [Michael Palin][/p?id=1661]. [De Niro][/p?id=1808] still wanted to be in the film, so he was cast as Tuttle instead.
  • [Jonathan Pryce's][/p?id=1508] role as Sam was written years earlier with him in mind. The character was originally designed to be in his mid-twenties ([Pryce][/p?id=1508] was only about 30 when [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] was developing the script), but after many years in limbo, [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] changed the character's age to mid-to-late thirties so that then-37-year-old [Pryce][/p?id=1508] could still play the role.
  • Director [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] was reported to have been rather unhappy with [Kim Greist's][/p?id=Kim%20Greist] performance, and as a result many of her scenes were drastically cut and/or trimmed down.
  • [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] had trouble with studio producers over the black ending he wanted on the film. The producers wanted a "happy Hollywood" film which eliminated (among other things) the final transition and a critical line of dialogue which reveals the fate of Jill. These changes were made, and this "butchered" version was shown on US television at least once. [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] threatened to disown the film, and consequently the cinematic release and all videotape versions show the film essentially as he intended it to be seen (although the US cinematic release still omitted the line about Jill).
  • During the climactic shootout at Information Retrieval, the janitor is killed and her vacuum cleaner rolls down the steps as the storm troopers walk and fire their weapons in a skirmish line formation. This is a reference to [Sergei M. Eisenstein's][/p?id=Sergei%20M.%20Eisenstein] film, [Bronenosets Potyomkin][/f?id=17330] (1925), when the Cossacks march down the steps of the Port of Odessa, firing away as a baby carriage rolls by.
  • This was [River Phoenix's][/p?id=1800] favorite movie, and he had been filming Dark Blood (1993) with [Jonathan Pryce][/p?id=1508]. As a gift, [Pryce][/p?id=1508] arranged for [Phoenix][/p?id=1800] to meet [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926], his hero. The meeting was set to happen the day he died outside the Viper Room. [Phoenix][/p?id=1800] never met him.
  • In the autumn of 1985, [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] and [Robert De Niro][/p?id=1808] appeared on "Good Morning America" (1975) to promote this film which was finished but not yet released. [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] was struggling with the studio and the studio head, Sid Sheinberg, quite publicly. [De Niro][/p?id=1808] rarely made television appearances to but agreed to help [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] out. According to [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] "Bobby [[De Niro][/p?id=1808]] said very little, he was talkative that day so we might have gotten him to ten words." Host [Joan Lunden][/p?id=Joan%20Lunden] asked [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] "I hear you're having trouble with the studio, is this correct?" [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] responded with "No, I'm having trouble with Sid Sheinberg, here is an 8x10 photo of him," and showed the entire nation his photograph. Sheinberg was reportedly furious with this incident, and it helped [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] get the release of the film done the way he wanted.
  • [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] was asked to do a film class during the battle of this film at USC. Terry agreed, and took advantage of the situation by preparing to bring an "audio visual aid", which was his cut of the film, which would have been allowed. Unfortunately, two days before the event, students advertised a free screening of the film. When he arrived it was announced that Universal would not allow him to show the film. During his speech to the class, he was interrupted by studio executives' phone calls. They eventually allowed him to show a clip of the film. He showed the entire film, and repeated the screenings for over two weeks. It was during one of these screenings that Los Angeles film critics saw the film, and awarded it the Best Picture of the Year award, which was responsible for getting the film released the way [Gilliam][/p?id=1926] wanted it.
  • According to [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] in the book "The Battle of [Brazil][/f?id=11299]", the toolbelt worn by Tuttle and all of its gadgets were supplied by [Robert De Niro][/p?id=1808] himself.
  • [Jack Purvis][/p?id=Jack%20Purvis], a regular in the films of [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] appears as "Dr. [Chapman][/p?id=1342]", a reference to fellow Python [Graham Chapman][/p?id=1342], who had a medical degree.
  • The samurai sequence was originally conceived to reflect [Terry Gilliam's][/p?id=1926] love for [Akira Kurosawa][/p?id=1007] films.
  • [Terry Gilliam][/p?id=1926] and his crew were excited to have [Robert De Niro][/p?id=1808] on board at first, but as time wore on they found [De Niro's][/p?id=1808] need for "research" and obsession with details increasingly irritating, saying that he "wanted to strangle him".
