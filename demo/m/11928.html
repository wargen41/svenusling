  • [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] regarded his performance here as the finest of his horror movie career.
  • The real Matthew Hopkins was only in his mid 20s in 1645 and died before he was 30. [Vincent Price's][/p?id=1978] character is middle-aged, like the actor himself. Hopkins and Stearne executed more than 300 people, mainly women, during their two or three years of '"witch hunting". Considering that 500 people in total were executed for witchcraft in England between the late 15th and late 18th centuries, it means that Hopkins was responsible for two-thirds of these executions during a period of three years.
  • Director [Michael Reeves][/p?id=Michael%20Reeves] wanted [Donald Pleasence][/p?id=1250] to play Matthew Hopkins, but American-International Pictures, the American distributor and co-financier of the film, insisted that [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] play the title character, and [Reeves][/p?id=Michael%20Reeves] grudgingly accepted.
  • Even the truncated version was met with considerable controversy by UK film critics. Dilys Powell in "The Sunday Times" complained, " . . . 17th-century hanging, burning, raping, screaming and [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] as England's prize torture-overseer. Peculiarly nauseating". "The Guardian" felt the film was filled with "gratuitous sadism". Margaret Hinxman of "The Sunday Telegraph" dismissed it as a "sadistic extravaganza". Nonetheless, several critics felt the film was worth accolades. John Russell Taylor in the "London Times Saturday Review" said the film " . . . is quite happily and deliberately a horror film: that is to say, it has no particular pretensions to being anything else . . . There is much in it which would win [Michael Reeves][/p?id=Michael%20Reeves] an important reputation if he were dealing with some more pretentious, but fundamentally no more serious subject . . . Mr. [Reeves][/p?id=Michael%20Reeves] is no longer merely promising. He already has real achievements behind him: not merely good horror films, but good films, period". Films and Filming noted that the film "has no explicit 'message', but it does say something about the springs of despair and it says it forcefully. It is a very frightening film . . . Matthew Hopkins is the best of [Price's][/p?id=1978] recent performances. [The film] is emphatically not a horror film; it is, however, a very horrifying one . . . ". Monthly Film Bulletin observed, "Not since [Peeping Tom][/f?id=Peeping%20Tom%20-%20En%20Smygtittare] (1960) has a film aroused such an outcry about nastiness and gratuitous violence as this one . . . the tone of the film is oddly muted, with torture and death in plenty, but viewed matter-of-factly and without stress . . . Throughout the whole film there is a vivid sense of a time out of joint, which comes as much from the stray groups of soldiers who skirmish against unseen attackers in the woods or hang wearily about by the wayside waiting for battle to commence, as from the bloody crimes committed in the name of religion by Matthew Hopkins".
  • Released as The Conqueror Worm in the US by American International Pictures, the film was given the title of a poem by Edgar Allan Poe, and an added voice of [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] reciting that poem, even though the film has nothing to do with any of Poe's writings.
  • As [Michael Reeves][/p?id=Michael%20Reeves] hadn't wanted [Vincent Price][/p?id=1978] for the main role of Matthew Hopkins, there was reportedly tension between the two of them while working together.
