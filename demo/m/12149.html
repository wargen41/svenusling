  • The backgrounds in the movie are actually superimposed photos of exotic tropical locations such as Tahiti and Hawaii.
  • The original screenplay featured no dialogue at all, but Michael Eisner of Disney insisted they include it.
  • At an official $130 million (unoffically $200 million) it is the most expensive movie released in 2000.
  • Water rippling in concentric circles with each footstep of an approaching predator parodies the famous shot in [Jurassic Park][/f?id=15341] (1993) and its sequel [The Lost World: Jurassic Park][/f?id=15815] (1997).
  • The original planned opening shot, showing the meteor moving through space, was cut when an identical opening shot was used in [Armageddon][/f?id=10463] (1998/I).
  • The job of composing the score was offered to [Harry Gregson-Williams][/p?id=Harry%20Gregson-Williams], but he turned it down Because of his work on [Shrek][/f?id=18326] (2001).
  • One original concept for the dialogue in the film would have seen the characters not moving their lips and beaks to talk but rather as voice-overs, similar to "Homeward Bound".
  • The elderly Brachiosaurus named Baylene was initially meant to be a much younger male Brachiosaurus named Sorbus who was described by the makers as a sort of [Chris Farley][/p?id=1159]-like character who had a fear of heights and so kept his head close to the ground. The character was revised to become Baylene, who, according to her official background story, was the last of a group of elderly Brachiosaurus.
  • One creature that was designed but cut from the final film was a Mosasaurus, a large, sea-dwelling reptile that would have been used in a scene where the Herd goes to a river to drink and encounters the Mosasaurus.
  • While lemurs appeared millions of years after the dinosaur era, the directors used them regardless because they felt the real mammals of the Cretaceous (the era in which the film takes place) were "hideous".
  • The plot hole pointed out by some as to why the Herd went to have their babies in the Nesting Grounds but didn't remain there (hence the desert exodus) was explained in the "Essential Guide" of the film: the land in the Nesting Grounds becomes too cold and infertile in the winter, driving the Herd to leave it and return when they begin to breed.
