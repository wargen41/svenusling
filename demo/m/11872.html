  • The script floated around Hollywood for nearly 20 years, during which time [Gene Hackman][/p?id=1317] read and rejected it, only to be later convinced by [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] (who had owned the rights to the script for some time) to play a role.
  • [Saul Rubinek][/p?id=1861] asks [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] how he chose the order in which to shoot six deputies. [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] replies that he "got lucky." This is a sly reference to [Eastwood's][/p?id=1184] earlier film [The Outlaw Josey Wales][/f?id=16030] (1976), in which [Chief Dan George][/p?id=Chief%20Dan%20George] asks [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] how he chose the order in which to shoot four Union soldiers, and [Eastwood][/p?id=1184] responds with a lengthy explanation about their various holsters and the looks in their eyes.
  • Only the third western to ever win the Best Picture Oscar. The other two being [Dances with Wolves][/f?id=11795] (1990) and [Cimarron][/f?id=Cimarron] (1931).
  • The final screen credit reads, "Dedicated to Sergio and Don", referring to [Clint Eastwood's][/p?id=1184] mentors, [Sergio Leone][/p?id=Sergio%20Leone] and '[Don Siegel][/p?id=Don%20Siegel]'.
  • It took [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] several years to actually get round to reading the script as his script reader had initially told him that it wasn't very good.
  • [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] said at the time that this would be the last movie that he would both act in and direct, but he went on to appear in quite a lot of movies he directed since then.
  • According to [Clint Eastwood][/p?id=1184] in a 2000 interview, [Gene Hackman][/p?id=1317] was very concerned about how they were going to show the violence in the movie, owing to the rising gun violence in American cities. [Eastwood][/p?id=1184], a lifelong supporter of gun control, assured [Hackman][/p?id=1317] that the film wouldn't glorify gun violence.
  • "The Duke" was a reference to [John Wayne][/p?id=1504].
