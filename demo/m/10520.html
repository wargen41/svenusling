  • Director [James Cameron][/p?id=1414] contacted Orson Scott Card before filming began with the possibility of producing a book based on the film. Card initially told his agent that he doesn't do "novelizations", but when she told him that the director was [James Cameron][/p?id=1414], he agreed to consider it. The script arrived, and Card signed on after receiving assurances from [Cameron][/p?id=1414] that he would be free to develop his "novel" the way he wanted to. After a meeting with [Cameron][/p?id=1414], Card immediately wrote the first three chapters, which dealt with events concerning Bud and Lindsay Brigman that occurred before the events in the film. [Cameron][/p?id=1414] gave these chapters to [Ed Harris][/p?id=1267] and [Mary Elizabeth Mastrantonio][/p?id=Mary%20Elizabeth%20Mastrantonio], who used it to develop their characters.
  • Cast members had to become certified divers before filming began.
  • The crew frequently spent enough time underwater to force them to undergo decompression before surfacing. [James Cameron][/p?id=1414] would often watch dailies through a glass window, while decompressing and hanging upside down to relieve the stress on his shoulders from the weight of the helmet.
  • Fluid breathing is a reality. Five rats were used for five different takes, all of whom survived and were given shots by a vet. The rat that actually appeared in the film died of natural causes a few weeks before the film opened. According to [James Cameron][/p?id=1414], the scene with the rat had to be edited out of the UK movie version because "the Royal Veterinarian felt that it was painful for the rat". [James Cameron][/p?id=1414] repeatedly assures that the rats used for this take didn't suffer any harm.
  • [James Cameron's][/p?id=1414] brother, [Mike Cameron][/p?id=Mike%20Cameron], plays a dead crewman inside the sunken submarine. To accomplish this he had to hold his breath under 15 feet of water while also allowing a crab to crawl out of his mouth.
  • During the rigorous and problematic shoot, the cast and crew began calling the film by various derogatory names such as "Son Of Abyss", "The Abuse" and "Life's Abyss And Then You Dive". [Mary Elizabeth Mastrantonio][/p?id=Mary%20Elizabeth%20Mastrantonio] reportedly suffered a physical and emotional breakdown because she was pushed so hard on the set, and [Ed Harris][/p?id=1267] had to pull over his car at one time while driving home, because he burst into spontaneous crying.
  • The studio pushed hard for an Academy Award nomination for [Michael Biehn][/p?id=1642] as best supporting actor.
  • The role of Commodore DeMarco was originally meant for [Lance Henriksen][/p?id=1565], but he couldn't appear due to a scheduling conflict.
  • The first feature film to have used an early version of Adobe Photoshop.
  • Filmed on a 70 million dollar budget.
  • Aside from [Michael Biehn][/p?id=1642], there are none of the usual regulars who work with [James Cameron][/p?id=1414] in The Abyss (1989). E.g. [Lance Henriksen][/p?id=1565], [Bill Paxton][/p?id=1083], [Jenette Goldstein][/p?id=Jenette%20Goldstein], etc.
  • During underwater filming, [James Cameron][/p?id=1414] almost drowned. At the bottom of the submerged set, [Cameron][/p?id=1414] ran out of air, Shedding his 40 pound helmet, he attempted to swim to the surface and was met by a safety diver, who grabbed [Cameron][/p?id=1414] and gave him a faulty regulator (water shot into [Cameron's][/p?id=1414] lungs). [Cameron][/p?id=1414] struggled and had to punch the diver to get free. Once safely out of the water, [Cameron][/p?id=1414] fired the diver and the Assistant Director (who had not warned [Cameron][/p?id=1414] he was running out of air), then had his helmet retrieved from the bottom of the set and continued working.
